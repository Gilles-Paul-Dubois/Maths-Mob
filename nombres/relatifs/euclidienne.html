We have a result similar to the in ℕ.
theorem
For all a ∈ ℤ, b ∈ ℤ*, there exists a unique pair of integers (q, r) with
a = bq + r and 0 ≤ r < |b|.

The proof is identical the results are the same when the numbers are positive but beware of negatives !
We have for example in the division by 3:

  • 11=3 × 3 + 2 q=3, r=2
  • -11= 3 × (-4) +1 q=-4, r=1

The concepts of of an integer remain. There is simply a 'symmetrization' of these sets.
As any relative integer is always divisible by the units -1 and +1, it follows that if, for example, the divisors of 12 in ℕ are {1,2,3,4,6,12}, the divisors of +12 (and also those of -12) will be in ℤ: { -12, -6, -4, -3, -2, -1,1,2,3,4,6,12}.

As in the case of ℕ:
a is divisible by b if and only if the remainder of the Euclidean division of a by b is zero.
