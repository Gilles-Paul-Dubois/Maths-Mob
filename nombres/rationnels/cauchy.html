Definition

The following definition may seem abstract, it is linked to a phenomenon that we will study later in detail and which is called 'convergence'. It characterizes some of rationals the terms of which tend to cluster as n increases. We will have need for this notion from the next chapter devoted to so-called 'real' numbers, but it is quite possible and even desirable to introduce it now.
definition
A sequence (u n) n∈ℕ of rational numbers is said to be a 'Cauchy sequence' if it satisfies the following criterion (said 'Cauchy's criterion' ):
∀ ε ∈ℚ, ∃ n 0 | m, n> n 0 ⇒ | u m -u n | <ε.
This means that however small s ε given in advance, for n big enough all terms of the suite are in a of length 2ε.

théorème
  • La suite extraite des termes d'ordre pair est décroissante u 0 < u 2 < u 4
  • La suite extraite des termes d'ordre impair est croissante u 1 > u 3 > u 5

Let us immediately point out a few particularities, the demonstration of which is immediate:
theorem
  • The sequence extracted from terms of even order is decreasing u 0 < u 2 < u 4
  • The sequence extracted from terms of odd order is increasing u 1 > u 3 > u 5
  • For all n, all u k for k >2n are in the interval ]u 2n+1,u 2n[ of length 1/2(n+1)
It follows that our sequence satisfies the Cauchy criterion, for ε given in advance it suffices to designate by N the smallest of the integers n such that 1/2(n+1) < ε then to take n 0 =2N.
