On établit ainsi un ensemble de conditions nécessaires pour l'existence de l'objet cherché, puis dans un second temps, on montre que ces conditions sont suffisantes. Notons que cette technique ne fonctionne pas dans tous les cas .

We will use here a classic constructivist method and apparently paradoxical which consists in 'assuming the problem solved' . We thus establish a set of conditions necessary for the existence of the object sought, then in a second time, we show that these conditions are sufficient. Note that this technique does not work in all cases .
Our problem is therefore to construct an extension of ℝ having at least one structure of and containing an element i satisfying i²=-1.
Call this ring K, K must at least contain ℝ[i] (see exercises on the reals concerning this notation), i.e. the set of polynomials in i with coefficients in ℝ. K must therefore at least contain the elements of the form a+bi (polynomials of the first degree).
Let a+bi and c+di ∈ K, K being a ring, we must have (a+bi)+(c+di)=(a+b)+(c+d)i. We must also have (a+bi)(c+di)=(ac+dbi²)+(ad+bc)i=(ac-db)+(ad+bc)i since i²=-1.

notation
From now on we will definitively abandon the writing of complexes in the form of pairs to keep only the 'usual' form a+bi.
definition
If z=a+bi, a is called the 'real part' of z and b is called the 'imaginary part' of z.
definition
A complex number whose real part is zero is said to be 'pure imaginary' .

A complex number whose part imaginary is zero is simply said to be 'real' .
