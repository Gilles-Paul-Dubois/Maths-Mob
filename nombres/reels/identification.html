Finally the important point (especially for calculus) is the fact that, contrary to ℚ
theorem
Any (resp. lower bounded) subset actually has an (resp lower bound).

This is easily seen on the UDEs.
Consider the case of a subset X contained for example in [0,1[, the elements therefore correspond to infinite sequences of decimals 0.a1a2a3.....an......
The first decimals of the elements of X have a maximum let us designate this maximum by b0.
The decimal seconds of the elements of X having b0 as the first decimal have themselves a maximum let us designate this maximum by b1.
The third decimals of the elements of X of the form 0.b0b1...... have a maximum let us denote this maximum by b2.
And so on.
Let b= 0.b1b2b3........ be the real number the UDE of which is made up of all the bi.
It is clear that it is an upper bound of X on the one hand and that we can find in X elements as close to b as we want, namely the decimals 0.b0, 0.b0b1, etc.
b therefore satisfies the definition of an upper bound for X.
