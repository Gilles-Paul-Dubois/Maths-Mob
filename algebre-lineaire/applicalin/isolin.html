Soit E un espace vectoriel de dimension finie et f: E → E un endomorphisme de E (application linéaire de E dans E) alors il y a équivalence entre:

  • f est bijective.
  • f est injective.
  • f est surjective.
  • f transforme TOUTE base de E en une base de E.
Cela nous fournit donc autant de caractérisations des endomorphismes bijectifs (automorphismes) d'un espace vectoriel de dimension finie.

Groupes linéaires

Commençons par une définition:
définition
Les isomorphismes de l'espace vectoriel E sur lui-même, encore appelés endomorphismes bijectifs ou automorphismes (voir cours bases des mathématiques), forment un groupe pour la composition. Ce groupe n'est pas abélien on l'appelle le 'groupe linéaire de E' et il est noté GL(E). C'est donc un sous-groupe du groupe de toutes les permutations de E.
Tout cela résulte simplement du fait que:
  • La composée de deux bijections est une bijection.
  • La composée de deux applications linéaires est une application linéaire.
  • La réciproque d'une application linéaire est linéaire (immédiat).
On voit tout de suite également que:
théorème
Si E et F sont isomorphes en tant qu'espaces vectoriels, GL(E) et GL(F) le sont en tant que groupes.
Cela se vérifie ainsi si φ: E → F est un isomorphisme. L'application f → φofoφ-1 est un isomorphisme de groupes de GL(E) sur GL(F). Un espace vectoriel de dimension n étant toujours isomorphe à Kn, dans le cas de la dimension finie le seul groupe linéaire qui importe est donc GL(Kn) que l'on note encore GL(n,K). Nous représenterons ce groupe dans le prochain chapitre.

General results

We have already seen a first characterization of that is :
f injective ⇔ = {0}
Now here's another one:
theorem
We assume that E is a and B=(u1,...,un) a of E, under these conditions:
f injective ⇔ the system (f(u1),....,f(un)) is

proof

Suppose indeed f(u)=0 with u= λ1u1+...+ λnun
We then have λ1f(u1)+...+λnf(u
n) = 0. If f(u1),....,f(un)) is linearly independent, we necessarily have λ1= ... =λn=0 so u = 0 and Ker(f)={0} so f is injective. Conversely suppose f injective and suppose that we have λ1f(u1)+...+λnf(un)=0, then we will have: f(λ1u1+...+λnun)=0 so λ1u1+...+λnun=0 therefore λ1=...=λn=0 since B=(u1,...,un) is a basis of E.
We also have another characterization of linear maps, that is :
f surjective ⇔ Im(f)=F
Here is another:
theorem
We assume that E is a finite dimensional vector space and B=(u1,...,un) a basis of E, under these conditions:
surjective f ⇔ the system (f(u1),....,f(un)) is

proof

Let f be surjective and let v be any vector in the destination space F. If (f(u1),....,f(un)) is spanning then v can be written as a linear combination of f(ui):
v= λ1f(u1)+...+λnf(un)
So by linearity:
v= f(λ1u1+...+λnun). it's as simple for the converse.
Combining the two previous results, we have:
theorem
If f: E → F is a linear map. There is equivalence between:
  • f is .
  • The image of any basis of E is a basis of F.
And we also have:
theorem
If f is a linear map of a from a finite dimensional space E to a finite dimensional space F with dim(E)=dim(F) for f to be an isomorphism, it suffices for f to be injective OR to be surjective.

proof

This results from the fact that in an n-dimensional space for n vectors to form a basis it suffices whether they form a free system or they form a spanning system.
The previous result therefore applies to endomorphisms of a finite dimensional vector space, and gives us:
theorem
Let E be a finite dimensional vector space and f: E → E an endomorphism of E (linear map from E to E) then there is equivalence between:
  • f transforms ONE basis of E into ONE basis of E.
  • f is bijective.
  • f is injective.
  • f is surjective.
  • f transforms ANY basis of E into a basis of E.
This therefore provides us with as many characterizations of bijective endomorphisms (automorphisms) of a vector space of finite dimension.

Linear groups

Let's start with a definition:
definition
The isomorphisms of the vector space E on itself, also called bijective endomorphisms or automorphisms (see basic mathematics course), form a group for composition. This group is not Abelian it is called the 'linear group of E' and it is denoted GL(E). It is therefore a subgroup of the group of all permutations of E.
All this simply results from the fact that:
  • The composite of two bijections is a bijection.
  • The composition of two linear maps is a linear map.
  • The inverse of a linear map is linear (immediate).
We also immediately see that:
theorem
If E and F are isomorphic as vector spaces, GL(E) and GL(F) are isomorphic as groups.
This is thus verified if φ: E → F is an isomorphism. The map f → φofoφ-1 is an isomorphism of groups from GL(E) onto GL(F). A vector space of dimension n being always isomorphic to Kn, in the case of the finite dimension the only linear group which matters is therefore GL(Kn ) which we still denote by GL(n,K). We will represent this group in the next chapter.
