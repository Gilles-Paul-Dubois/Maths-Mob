Intro

that the fundamental idea of differential calculus was the local approximation of functions by affine functions (polynomials of the first degree). We will now generalize this with polynomial functions of degree >1. We will see that a sufficient condition for this to be possible is that the function has, at the point where it is studied, derivatives of higher order. The authorship of these results is attributed to Brook Taylor, but it seems that James Gregory was already familiar with these techniques before him. Cauchy, Laplace and Lagrange came to refine these results with regard to the evaluation of the 'remainder' (the error committed by locally replacing the function by a polynomial). We introduce here some notations that will be used throughout the chapter.
I denotes an open interval and a ∈ I a point of I.
n denotes an integer n ≥ 1.
f designates a numerical function defined on I (therefore in the neighborhood of a), and it is assumed that f is n times differentiable at a, its successive derivatives at a being f(0)(a)=f (a) , f(1)(a)=f'(a), , f(2)(a)=f"(a), etc... up to f(n)(a) n-th derivative of f at point a.
définition 1
On désigne par T(n,f,a) la fonction polynomiale de degré n définie sur I par:

\( \displaystyle T(n,f,a)(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}f^{(k)}(a)\frac{(x-a)^{k}}{k!} \)

Ce polynôme sera parfois appelé "l'approximation de Taylor" d'ordre n de la fonction f, au point a.
Enfin on désigne par R(n,f,a) la différence f-T(n,f,a), soit:
R(n,f,a)(x)=f(x)-T(n,f,a)(x) ∀ x ∈ I.
R(n,f,a) sera lui-même désigné sous le non de 'reste' de Taylor.
definition 1
We denote by T(n,f,a) the polynomial function of degree n defined on I by:

\( \displaystyle T(n,f,a)(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}f^{(k)}(a)\frac{(x-a)^{k}}{k! } \)

This polynomial will sometimes be called " n-th order Taylor expansion" of the function f, at point a.
Finally, we denote by R(n,f,a) the difference f-T(n,f,a), i.e.:
R(n,f,a)(x)=f(x)-T(n,f,a)(x) ∀ x ∈ I.
R(n,f,a) will itself be referred to as Taylor's 'remainder' .
Lorsque n et a seront supposés connus et fixes on allégera un peu les notations en notant simplement T au lieu de T(n,f,a) et R au lieu de R(n,f,a). La plupart des théorèmes qui suivent disent que T est une estimation de f au voisinage de a d'autant meilleure que n est elevé. Les différents énoncés donnent une estimation du reste qui représente donc l'erreur commise en remplaçant f par T.
